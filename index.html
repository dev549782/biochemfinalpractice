Module 1: Thermodynamics

  1. Which of the following reactions is spontaneous?
    • A) A + B ⇌ C  ΔG = 32 kJ/mol
    • B) A + D ⇌ C + B  ΔG = 5 kJ/mol
    • C) A ⇌ C + E  ΔG = –12 kJ/mol
    • D) B + F ⇌ G  ΔG = +8 kJ/mol
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Only option C has a negative ΔG value, indicating the reaction is spontaneous.

  2. Under standard conditions, how many steps in glycolysis are spontaneous?
    • A) 0
    • B) 2
    • C) 5
    • D) Most of them
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: Glycolysis has several steps with large negative ΔG°′ values, making most of them spontaneous under standard biological conditions.

  3. How much of A is converted to B when ΔG = 0?
    • A) 10%
    • B) 25%
    • C) 50%
    • D) 90%
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: When ΔG = 0, the system is at equilibrium, meaning forward and reverse reactions occur equally. Thus, 50% conversion occurs.

  4. Which factors affect equilibrium for the reaction A + B ⇌ C?
    • A) Reactant concentration
    • B) Product concentration
    • C) Temperature
    • D) All of the above
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: Le Chatelier’s Principle says equilibrium is influenced by changes in concentration, pressure, and temperature.

  5. G6P ⇌ F6P has ΔG° = +1.7 kJ/mol. What is favored at equilibrium?
    • A) All G6P is converted to F6P
    • B) There is more G6P than F6P
    • C) There is more F6P than G6P
    • D) No F6P is formed
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: A positive ΔG° means the reaction is not favorable under standard conditions, so G6P is favored at equilibrium.

  6. A reaction at 27°C has ΔH = +20.0 kJ/mol and ΔS = +20.0 J/K•mol. Is it enthalpically or entropically driven?
    • A) Enthalpically driven
    • B) Entropically driven
    • C) Neither
    • D) Cannot be determined
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Since ΔH is positive (unfavorable) but ΔS is positive, entropy is driving the reaction forward at higher temperatures.

  7. What is Keq for ΔH = +20.0 kJ/mol and ΔS = +20.0 J/K•mol at 27°C?
    • A) 0.00365
    • B) 274
    • C) 1.22 × 10²⁷
    • D) Cannot determine
    Show Answer

    Answer: A
    Explanation: Use ΔG = ΔH – TΔS and then ΔG = –RT ln Keq to solve for Keq.

  8. What is the oxidation state of carbon in methanol (CH₃OH)?
    • A) +1
    • B) 0
    • C) –1
    • D) –3
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Carbon balances the +1 (hydrogens) and –2 (oxygen) for a net zero charge. So carbon must be –1.

  9. What is the ΔG° for oxidation of NADH by O₂?
    • A) –96.5 kJ/mol
    • B) +96.5 kJ/mol
    • C) –218 kJ/mol
    • D) +218 kJ/mol
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: ΔE° = 0.815 – (–0.315) = 1.13 V, and ΔG = –nFΔE° = –2 × 96.5 × 1.13 = –218 kJ/mol.

  10. Which of the following reactions is entropically favored?
    • A) C₂H₅OH(l) ⇌ C₂H₅OH(g)
    • B) Na₂CO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
    • C) 2Al(s) + 3Cl₂(g) → 2AlCl₃(s)
    • D) A and B
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: A and B both increase the number of gas molecules or disorder, indicating a favorable entropy change.

Module 2: Non-Covalent Interactions

  1. When two hydrogen atoms react to form H₂ gas, the reaction is:
    • A) Exothermic — bond formation releases heat
    • B) Endothermic — bond formation absorbs heat
    • C) Exothermic — bond formation absorbs heat
    • D) Endothermic — bond formation releases heat
    Show Answer

    Answer: A
    Explanation: Bond formation always releases energy — making this an exothermic process.

  2. Which bond is strongest in aqueous solution?
    • A) Ionic bond
    • B) Covalent bond
    • C) Hydrogen bond
    • D) Van der Waals force
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Covalent bonds involve electron sharing and are far stronger than any non-covalent interactions in water.

  3. How many hydrogen bonds can one urea molecule donate?
    • A) 2
    • B) 3
    • C) 4
    • D) 5
    Show Answer

    Answer: A
    Explanation: Urea has two NH₂ groups, with two hydrogen bond donors total (one per NH group).

  4. How many hydrogen bonds can one urea molecule accept?
    • A) 1
    • B) 2
    • C) 3
    • D) 4
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Urea’s carbonyl oxygen and lone pairs on both nitrogen atoms allow for three H-bond acceptor positions.

  5. Which interaction best describes a positively charged amine and a carboxylate?
    • A) Hydrogen bond
    • B) London dispersion force
    • C) Dipole-dipole interaction
    • D) Ionic interaction
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: This is a classic electrostatic (ionic) interaction between two full charges of opposite signs.

  6. Why do oil and water separate in solution?
    • A) Oil is polar
    • B) Water forms hydrogen bonds with oil
    • C) Entropic driving force
    • D) Ionic repulsion
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Water forms ordered cages around nonpolar molecules, reducing entropy. Separation restores disorder (ΔS ↑).

  7. What kind of interaction occurs between water and Na⁺?
    • A) Dipole-dipole
    • B) Hydrogen bonding
    • C) Ion-dipole
    • D) Van der Waals
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: The partial negative charge on oxygen in water stabilizes the positively charged sodium ion via ion-dipole interaction.

  8. The dissolution of NaCl in water (ΔH = +4 kJ/mol) is:
    • A) Enthalpically driven
    • B) Entropically driven
    • C) Equilibrium-driven
    • D) Not spontaneous
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Dissolution increases disorder (ΔS > 0), which drives spontaneity despite positive ΔH.

  9. Which buffer is most suitable for an enzyme with optimal pH 8.5?
    • A) Citrate (pKa = 4.7)
    • B) MOPS (pKa = 7.2)
    • C) Tris (pKa = 8.2)
    • D) CHES (pKa = 9.55)
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: CHES is closest in pKa to 8.5 and best buffers near that pH.

  10. Which is NOT a reason oil and water separate?
    • A) “Like dissolves like”
    • B) Strong intermolecular interactions within oil
    • C) Water avoids forming cages around nonpolar molecules
    • D) Entropic driving force
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: The main driver is water avoiding low-entropy “cages,” not strong internal bonding in oil.

Module 3: Amino Acids & Primary Protein Structure

  1. Which amino acid has a sulfur atom in its side chain?
    • A) Proline
    • B) Methionine
    • C) Histidine
    • D) Tryptophan
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Methionine contains a thioether (-S-CH₃) group in its side chain.

  2. Which of the following has an aromatic side chain?
    • A) Glycine
    • B) Isoleucine
    • C) Histidine
    • D) Tryptophan
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: Tryptophan has a large indole ring, which is aromatic.

  3. Which amino acid is most likely to act as a buffer at physiological pH?
    • A) Glutamic acid
    • B) Arginine
    • C) Tyrosine
    • D) Histidine
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: Histidine's pKa is ~6.0, close to physiological pH, so it buffers well in cells.

  4. Which amino acid is nonpolar?
    • A) Glutamine
    • B) Histidine
    • C) Serine
    • D) Isoleucine
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: Isoleucine has a hydrophobic aliphatic side chain, making it nonpolar.

  5. Which amino acid has a basic side chain?
    • A) Aspartic acid
    • B) Serine
    • C) Lysine
    • D) Asparagine
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Lysine has a terminal amine with a high pKa (~10.5), making it basic.

  6. Which bonds in a polypeptide backbone are rotatable?
    • A) N–Cα only
    • B) Cα–C only
    • C) Peptide bond only
    • D) Both N–Cα and Cα–C
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: The N–Cα and Cα–C bonds are rotatable. The peptide bond (C–N) is planar due to partial double bond character.

  7. What is the isoelectric point (pI) of glycine (pKa₁ = 2.35, pKa₂ = 9.78)?
    • A) 3.0
    • B) 5.0
    • C) 6.07
    • D) 7.5
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: pI = (2.35 + 9.78)/2 = 6.07

  8. What is the charge of arginine at pH 10.5 (pKa₁ = 1.8, pKa₂ = 9.0, pKaR = 12.5)?
    • A) +2
    • B) +1
    • C) 0
    • D) –1
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: COO– = –1, NH₃⁺ = 0, guanidinium group = +1 → net charge = +1

  9. What is the pI of the tetrapeptide NHEK?
    • A) 3
    • B) 5
    • C) 7
    • D) 9
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: The basic and acidic residues roughly cancel near neutral pH, giving a pI ≈ 7.

  10. Which of these amino acids can form hydrogen bonds via side chain donation?
    • A) Isoleucine
    • B) Phenylalanine
    • C) Threonine
    • D) Glycine
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Threonine has an –OH group capable of donating a hydrogen bond.

Module 4: Protein Purification

  1. What does electrospray ionization mass spectrometry (ESI-MS) measure?
    • A) Total charge
    • B) Mass only
    • C) Volume
    • D) Mass-to-charge ratio (m/z)
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: ESI-MS separates ions based on their mass-to-charge (m/z) ratio, a key analytical method in proteomics.

  2. How many positive ion peaks should be observed in the ESI-MS of the tetrapeptide NHEK?
    • A) 1
    • B) 2
    • C) 3
    • D) 4
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Three ionizable groups (N-terminus, His, Lys) give rise to three protonated species detectable in ESI-MS.

  3. What method separates proteins by molecular size?
    • A) Ion exchange chromatography
    • B) Affinity chromatography
    • C) Size exclusion chromatography
    • D) Hydrophobic interaction chromatography
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Size exclusion chromatography separates molecules based on size, with larger molecules eluting first.

  4. The hydrophobic effect is most important in which purification methods?
    • A) Salting out
    • B) Hydrophobic interaction chromatography
    • C) Size exclusion chromatography
    • D) A and B
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: Both "salting out" and hydrophobic interaction chromatography exploit hydrophobic interactions to separate proteins.

  5. Which buffer ensures that NHEK binds to an anion exchange column (pI = 7)?
    • A) Citrate (pKa = 4.7)
    • B) MOPS (pKa = 7.2)
    • C) Tris (pKa = 8.2)
    • D) CHES (pKa = 9.5)
    Show Answer

    Answer: A
    Explanation: Using a buffer with pH below the pI ensures that the peptide is deprotonated and negatively charged, allowing it to bind anion exchangers.

  6. Which peptide is least likely to bind to an anion exchange column at pH 7.0?
    • A) Peptide I (pI = 6.28)
    • B) Peptide II (pI = 4.53)
    • C) Peptide III (pI = 9.24)
    • D) Peptides II and III
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: A peptide with pI > pH 7.0 will be positively charged and will not bind to the negatively charged resin of an anion exchange column.

  7. What is the elution order on a cation exchange column at pH 7.0 for peptides with pIs of 6.28, 4.27, and 9.24?
    • A) I, II, then III
    • B) II, I, then III
    • C) III, I, then II
    • D) III, II, then I
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Peptide II is least positively charged and elutes first, followed by I, then III which binds most tightly.

  8. If β-mercaptoethanol is left out during SDS-PAGE of insulin, what is the result?
    • A) Insulin runs as a single monomer
    • B) Disulfide bonds remain intact
    • C) Insulin appears larger
    • D) Both B and C
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: Without β-ME, disulfide bonds hold insulin chains together, making it run as a higher-mass complex.

  9. What does a chromatogram show in protein purification?
    • A) Protein identity
    • B) UV absorbance per fraction
    • C) SDS-PAGE analysis
    • D) pH gradient
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: A chromatogram plots UV absorbance (typically at 280 nm) vs. elution volume, showing how much protein is in each fraction.

  10. Which method best separates peptides based on isoelectric points?
    • A) SDS-PAGE
    • B) Size exclusion chromatography
    • C) Ion exchange chromatography
    • D) Hydrophobic interaction chromatography
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Ion exchange chromatography separates proteins based on their charge, which is influenced by their pI relative to buffer pH.

Module 5: 3D Protein Structure

  1. Which level of protein structure is primarily determined by the amino acid sequence?
    • A. Secondary
    • B. Tertiary
    • C. Primary
    • D. Quaternary
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: The primary structure is defined by the linear sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain, held together by covalent peptide bonds.

  2. What type of interaction mainly stabilizes secondary protein structures like alpha-helices and beta-sheets?
    • A. Covalent bonds
    • B. Disulfide bridges
    • C. Hydrogen bonds
    • D. Ionic interactions
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Hydrogen bonds between the peptide backbone stabilize the alpha-helices and beta-sheets in secondary structure.

  3. Which of the following structures is most stabilized by the hydrophobic effect?
    • A. Primary
    • B. Secondary
    • C. Tertiary
    • D. None
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Tertiary structure is stabilized by interactions among side chains, including the hydrophobic effect which drives nonpolar residues toward the protein’s interior.

  4. What causes the trans conformation of peptide bonds to be more stable than the cis conformation?
    • A. Hydrogen bonding
    • B. Steric hindrance
    • C. Disulfide bonds
    • D. Ionic repulsion
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Steric hindrance between adjacent side chains makes the trans conformation more stable (by ~8 kJ/mol).

  5. Which of the following would most likely cause a protein to denature?
    • A. Sodium dodecyl sulfate
    • B. Ammonium sulfate
    • C. Low pH
    • D. All of the above
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: All these conditions disrupt non-covalent interactions, causing protein unfolding or denaturation.

  6. Which configuration around a peptide bond is more common and stable?
    • A. Cis
    • B. Trans
    • C. Gauche
    • D. Planar
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Trans peptide bonds are more stable due to reduced steric clashes between side chains.

  7. In a Ramachandran plot, which constraints determine possible phi (Φ) and psi (Ψ) angles?
    • A. Backbone torsional strain
    • B. Steric hindrance
    • C. Hydrogen bonding patterns
    • D. A and B
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: Both steric hindrance and torsional constraints of the peptide backbone limit allowed angles.

  8. Which secondary structure element is most stabilized by intra-chain hydrogen bonding?
    • A. Beta sheet
    • B. Alpha helix
    • C. Beta turn
    • D. Random coil
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Alpha helices are stabilized by hydrogen bonds formed between every fourth amino acid within the chain.

  9. Which of the following residues is most likely to be found in the interior of a folded protein?
    • A. Aspartic acid (D)
    • B. Arginine (R)
    • C. Phenylalanine (F)
    • D. Serine (S)
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Phenylalanine is nonpolar and hydrophobic, making it more likely to be buried in the protein’s interior.

  10. What structural level requires more than one polypeptide chain?
    • A. Primary
    • B. Secondary
    • C. Tertiary
    • D. Quaternary
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: Quaternary structure describes the spatial arrangement of multiple polypeptide subunits.

Module 6: Protein Structure & Function

  1. Which parameter is most directly related to activation energy?
    • A. Equilibrium constants
    • B. Reactant concentrations
    • C. Product concentrations
    • D. Rate constants
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: The rate constant (k) is exponentially related to activation energy via the Arrhenius equation.

  2. Which molecule binds to myoglobin with the highest affinity?
    • A. Dioxygen (O₂)
    • B. Carbon monoxide (CO)
    • C. Nitric oxide (NO)
    • D. Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S)
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: NO has the highest Ka (1.4×10¹¹ M⁻¹), indicating strongest binding to myoglobin.

  3. Why does CO bind well to free heme but not to myoglobin?
    • A. CO forms more hydrogen bonds with myoglobin
    • B. Myoglobin destabilizes CO binding via sterics
    • C. CO prefers to bind to metal ions without proteins
    • D. Myoglobin lacks a heme group
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: His E7 in myoglobin causes a steric clash with CO and favors O₂'s bent conformation.

  4. What is the effect of mutating His E7 to Gly in myoglobin?
    • A. Increases CO binding
    • B. Increases O₂ binding
    • C. Decreases O₂ binding
    • D. No effect
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: The His E7 stabilizes O₂ via H-bonding. Removing it weakens O₂ binding affinity.

  5. Which of the following explains the difference in O₂ binding curves of hemoglobin vs. myoglobin?
    • A. Myoglobin has a distal histidine that hemoglobin lacks
    • B. Hemoglobin has subunit communication; myoglobin does not
    • C. Myoglobin binds O₂ cooperatively
    • D. Hemoglobin does not bind O₂ in tissues
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Hemoglobin is a tetramer that exhibits cooperativity through subunit communication.

  6. What is the Hill coefficient (n) for myoglobin?
    • A. 0
    • B. 1
    • C. 2
    • D. 3
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Myoglobin is a monomer and does not exhibit cooperativity (n = 1).

  7. At 30 torr O₂, what is the saturation of myoglobin and hemoglobin, respectively?
    • A. 0.60, 0.91
    • B. 0.91, 0.60
    • C. 2.8 torr, 26 torr
    • D. 0.97, 0.91
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Myoglobin (p50 = 2.8) is nearly saturated at 30 torr; hemoglobin (p50 = 26) is ~60%.

  8. How does increasing pH by 1 unit affect hemoglobin’s T-to-R equilibrium?
    • A. Stabilizes T-state
    • B. Stabilizes R-state
    • C. Has no effect
    • D. Causes denaturation
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Higher pH removes H⁺ from ion pairs, destabilizing T-state and favoring R-state (Bohr effect).

  9. What is the role of BPG (2,3-bisphosphoglycerate) in hemoglobin function?
    • A. None
    • B. Stabilizes T-state to promote O₂ dissociation
    • C. Stabilizes T-state to promote O₂ association
    • D. Stabilizes R-state to promote O₂ association
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: BPG binds the T-state and reduces O₂ affinity, aiding O₂ release in tissues.

  10. How many of the side chain nitrogens in His146 of hemoglobin are protonated at physiological pH?
    • A. 0
    • B. 1
    • C. 2
    • D. 3
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: One nitrogen of His is typically protonated near physiological pH, enabling ionic interactions.

Module 7: Catalysis, Rates, and Enzymes

  1. Hydrolysis of peptide bonds has ΔG° = –15 kJ/mol. What does this indicate?
    • A. Reaction is at equilibrium
    • B. Reactant-favored
    • C. Product-favored
    • D. Cannot determine
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: A negative ΔG° means the products (hydrolyzed peptides) are thermodynamically favored.

  2. For 2A + B → C (elementary), what is the rate law?
    • A. Rate = k[A][B]
    • B. Rate = k[B]
    • C. Rate = k[A]²[B]
    • D. Rate = k[A]²
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: In an elementary reaction, the stoichiometry matches the rate law directly.

  3. What are the units of k for Rate = k[A]²[B]?
    • A. s⁻¹
    • B. M⁻¹s⁻¹
    • C. M⁻²s⁻¹
    • D. M·s⁻¹
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: The rate has units of M/s. For 3rd order, k must have units of M⁻²s⁻¹ to balance.

  4. What is the initial velocity if k = 8×10³ s⁻¹ and [A] = 6×10⁻² M (first order)?
    • A. 1.33×10⁵ M/s
    • B. 4.8×10² M/s
    • C. 7.5×10⁻² M/s
    • D. Not enough data
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Rate = k[A] = (8×10³)(6×10⁻²) = 480 M/s.

  5. Why are intramolecular reactions typically faster than intermolecular ones?
    • A. Increased enthalpy
    • B. Greater ΔG
    • C. Improved proximity and orientation
    • D. Higher pressure
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: The proximity and alignment of groups in intramolecular reactions reduce entropy loss.

  6. Which mutation would most likely reduce chymotrypsin's affinity for substrate?
    • A. Ser189 → F
    • B. Gly216 → V
    • C. Ser189 → G
    • D. Ser189 → A
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Mutating Ser189 to glycine disrupts the binding pocket that interacts with aromatic residues.

  7. What interactions stabilize the transition state of a serine protease?
    • A. Electrostatic attraction
    • B. Hydrogen bonds
    • C. Electron repulsion
    • D. A and B
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: Electrostatic and H-bond interactions stabilize the negatively charged transition state.

  8. What happens if Asp102 is mutated to Lys in the protease active site?
    • A. No change in activity
    • B. Increased nucleophilicity of Ser195
    • C. Enzyme activity increases
    • D. Enzyme activity decreases
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: Asp102 is essential for forming the catalytic triad and activating Ser195.

  9. What is the role of His119 in RNase A?
    • A. Nucleophile
    • B. General base
    • C. Cofactor
    • D. Allosteric regulator
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: His119 abstracts a proton, making it a general base to initiate catalysis.

  10. What is the effect of mutating His119 to Asn in RNase A?
    • A. Increases rate
    • B. Rate stays the same
    • C. Rate decreases
    • D. Enzyme denatures
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Asn cannot act as a base, so the catalytic efficiency of RNase A drops significantly.
