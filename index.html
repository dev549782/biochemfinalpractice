Module 1: Thermodynamics

1.1. Which of the following reactions is spontaneous?

Answer: C) A ⇌ C + E
Explanation: Spontaneity corresponds to a negative ΔG. Only reaction C has ΔG = -12 kJ/mol.

1.2. How many steps are spontaneous under standard biological conditions if ΔG°′(glycolysis) = -79.9 kJ/mol?

Answer: All steps that contribute negatively to ΔG.
Explanation: Most glycolytic steps have negative ΔG°′ values, indicating spontaneity.

1.3. How much of A is converted to B if ΔG = 0?

Answer: 50%
Explanation: At equilibrium (ΔG = 0), the forward and reverse reactions occur at equal rates.

1.4. What affects the equilibrium of A(g) + B(g) ⇌ C(g)?

Answer: E) All of the above
Explanation: Le Chatelier's principle: concentration, temperature, and pressure all impact equilibrium.

1.5. G6P ⇌ F6P with ΔG° = +1.7. What's favored at equilibrium?

Answer: B) More G6P than F6P
Explanation: Positive ΔG° means the reaction favors reactants under standard conditions.

1.6. Is ΔG = +20 and ΔS = +20 J/K•mol enthalpically or entropically driven?

Answer: B) Entropically driven
Explanation: Since ΔH is positive and ΔS is positive, the spontaneity depends on temperature. At high T, the -TΔS term dominates.

1.7. What is Keq for ΔH = +20 and ΔS = +20 at 27°C?

Answer: A) 0.00365
Explanation: Use ΔG = ΔH - TΔS, then ΔG = -RTlnKeq. Solve accordingly.

1.8. What is the oxidation state of carbon in CH₃OH?

Answer: C) -1
Explanation: Assign hydrogen as +1 and oxygen as -2, and solve for carbon.

1.9. What is the ΔG° for the oxidation of NADH by O₂?

Answer: C) -218 kJ/mol
Explanation: ΔE° = 0.815 - (-0.315) = 1.13 V. ΔG° = -nFΔE° = -2×96.5×1.13

1.10. Which of the following reactions is entropically favored?

Answer: D) A and B
Explanation: Reactions producing more gas molecules increase entropy.

Module 2: Non-Covalent Interactions

2.1. When two hydrogen atoms form H₂, what type of reaction is it?

Answer: A) Exothermic
Explanation: Bond formation releases energy, so this is an exothermic reaction.

2.2. Which type of bond is strongest in aqueous solution?

Answer: B) Covalent bond
Explanation: Covalent bonds involve shared electrons and are stronger than non-covalent interactions.

2.3. How many hydrogen bonds can one molecule of urea donate?

Answer: A) 2
Explanation: Urea has two NH₂ groups; each hydrogen on nitrogen can serve as a hydrogen bond donor.

2.4. How many hydrogen bonds can one urea molecule accept?

Answer: C) 3
Explanation: Urea’s carbonyl oxygen and two nitrogen lone pairs can act as acceptors.

2.5. What is the main interaction between a positively charged amine and a carboxylate group?

Answer: Ionic (electrostatic) interaction
Explanation: Opposite full charges attract in ionic bonds, especially between basic and acidic side chains.

2.6. Why do oil and water separate in solution?

Answer: C) Entropic driving force
Explanation: Water cages around nonpolar molecules reduce entropy, so separation increases entropy.

2.7. What kind of interaction is formed between water and Na⁺?

Answer: Ion-dipole interaction
Explanation: Water’s partial negative oxygen interacts with the positive ion to form a solvation shell.

2.8. The dissolution of NaCl in water is:

Answer: B) Entropically driven
Explanation: ΔH is positive, but the process increases entropy as ions become more disordered in solution.

2.9. What is the best buffer for an enzyme with optimal activity at pH 8.5?

Answer: D) CHES (pKa = 9.55)
Explanation: Buffers are most effective near their pKa ± 1. CHES has a pKa closest to 8.5.

2.10. Which of the following is NOT a reason oil and water separate?

Answer: B) Strong intermolecular forces within oil
Explanation: The separation is driven mainly by entropy, not strong attractions within the oil phase.

Module 3: Amino Acids & Primary Structure

3.1. Which of the following amino acids has a side chain that can act as a hydrogen bond acceptor?

Answer: A) Threonine (T)
Explanation: The OH group on threonine can accept a hydrogen bond via the lone pairs on the oxygen.

3.2. Which of these amino acids has a sulfur atom in its side chain?

Answer: B) Methionine (M)
Explanation: Methionine has a thioether group (-S-CH3) in its side chain.

3.3. Which of the following amino acids has an aromatic side chain?

Answer: D) Tryptophan (W)
Explanation: Tryptophan contains a double-ring system with delocalized electrons, classifying it as aromatic.

3.4. Which amino acid has a side chain with a pKa of ~6 and can act as a buffer at physiological pH?

Answer: D) Histidine (H)
Explanation: Histidine's imidazole side chain has a pKa near 6, making it an effective physiological buffer.

3.5. How many hydrogen bond donors does the side chain of histidine have at pH 8.0?

Answer: 1
Explanation: At pH 8.0, the side chain is mostly unprotonated with only one nitrogen donating a hydrogen bond.

3.6. What is the isoelectric point (pI) of glycine with pKa1 = 2.35 and pKa2 = 9.78?

Answer: 6.07
Explanation: pI = (pKa1 + pKa2)/2 = (2.35 + 9.78)/2 = 6.07

3.7. What is the net charge of arginine at pH 10.5 (pKa1 = 1.8, pKa2 = 9.0, pKaR = 12.5)?

Answer: +1
Explanation: Carboxyl (-1), amino (0), guanidinium (+1); total = +1

3.8. Which of these amino acids is nonpolar?

Answer: E) Isoleucine (I)
Explanation: Isoleucine has a branched alkyl side chain, making it hydrophobic and nonpolar.

3.9. Which peptide bond(s) are rotatable in a polypeptide chain?

Answer: D) I and II
Explanation: N–Cα and Cα–C bonds are rotatable; the peptide bond itself (C–N) is planar and not rotatable.

3.10. What is the estimated pI of the tetrapeptide NHEK?

Answer: D) 7
Explanation: The side chains (His ~6.0, Glu ~4.1, Lys ~10.5) balance around neutral pH, giving a pI near 7.

Module 4: Protein Purification

4.1. What does electrospray ionization mass spectrometry (ESI-MS) measure?

Answer: D) Mass/charge
Explanation: ESI-MS separates ionized molecules based on their mass-to-charge (m/z) ratio.

4.2. How many positive ion peaks should be observed in the ESI-MS of tetrapeptide NHEK?

Answer: C) 3
Explanation: The N-terminal, His, and Lys can all be protonated under ESI conditions, giving 3 charge states.

4.3. What purification method is based on size?

Answer: C) Size exclusion chromatography
Explanation: This technique separates molecules based on their hydrodynamic volume (size).

4.4. Which purification methods rely on the hydrophobic effect?

Answer: D) Both A and B
Explanation: “Salting out” and hydrophobic interaction chromatography both exploit hydrophobicity.

4.5. Which buffer should be used to ensure NHEK binds to an anion exchange column?

Answer: A) Citrate
Explanation: To bind to an anion exchange column, NHEK must be negatively charged; citrate has a low enough pH to ensure this.

4.6. Which peptide is least likely to bind to an anion exchange column at pH 7.0?

Answer: C) Peptide III (pI = 9.24)
Explanation: If the peptide’s pI > pH, it’s positively charged and won’t bind to an anion exchanger (which attracts negatives).

4.7. Predict the order of elution on a cation exchange column at pH 7: Peptides I (pI = 6.28), II (pI = 4.27), III (pI = 9.24)

Answer: B) II, I, then III
Explanation: At pH 7, peptide III is most positively charged and binds strongest; II is most negative and elutes first.

4.8. What happens if β-mercaptoethanol is left out during SDS-PAGE of insulin?

Answer: Disulfide bonds are not reduced
Explanation: Without β-ME, disulfide bonds remain intact and insulin may migrate as a dimer or higher-order complex.

4.9. What does a chromatogram measure in protein purification?

Answer: Concentration of protein in fractions
Explanation: As proteins elute, their absorbance is measured to identify peaks corresponding to separated proteins.

4.10. What is the best non-denaturing method to separate peptides differing in pI?

Answer: A) Ion exchange chromatography
Explanation: Ion exchange exploits charge differences, which depend on the peptide’s isoelectric point.

Module 5: 3D Protein Structure

  1. Which level of protein structure is primarily determined by the amino acid sequence?
    • A. Secondary
    • B. Tertiary
    • C. Primary
    • D. Quaternary
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: The primary structure is defined by the linear sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain, held together by covalent peptide bonds.

  2. What type of interaction mainly stabilizes secondary protein structures like alpha-helices and beta-sheets?
    • A. Covalent bonds
    • B. Disulfide bridges
    • C. Hydrogen bonds
    • D. Ionic interactions
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Hydrogen bonds between the peptide backbone stabilize the alpha-helices and beta-sheets in secondary structure.

  3. Which of the following structures is most stabilized by the hydrophobic effect?
    • A. Primary
    • B. Secondary
    • C. Tertiary
    • D. None
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Tertiary structure is stabilized by interactions among side chains, including the hydrophobic effect which drives nonpolar residues toward the protein’s interior.

  4. What causes the trans conformation of peptide bonds to be more stable than the cis conformation?
    • A. Hydrogen bonding
    • B. Steric hindrance
    • C. Disulfide bonds
    • D. Ionic repulsion
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Steric hindrance between adjacent side chains makes the trans conformation more stable (by ~8 kJ/mol).

  5. Which of the following would most likely cause a protein to denature?
    • A. Sodium dodecyl sulfate
    • B. Ammonium sulfate
    • C. Low pH
    • D. All of the above
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: All these conditions disrupt non-covalent interactions, causing protein unfolding or denaturation.

  6. Which configuration around a peptide bond is more common and stable?
    • A. Cis
    • B. Trans
    • C. Gauche
    • D. Planar
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Trans peptide bonds are more stable due to reduced steric clashes between side chains.

  7. In a Ramachandran plot, which constraints determine possible phi (Φ) and psi (Ψ) angles?
    • A. Backbone torsional strain
    • B. Steric hindrance
    • C. Hydrogen bonding patterns
    • D. A and B
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: Both steric hindrance and torsional constraints of the peptide backbone limit allowed angles.

  8. Which secondary structure element is most stabilized by intra-chain hydrogen bonding?
    • A. Beta sheet
    • B. Alpha helix
    • C. Beta turn
    • D. Random coil
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Alpha helices are stabilized by hydrogen bonds formed between every fourth amino acid within the chain.

  9. Which of the following residues is most likely to be found in the interior of a folded protein?
    • A. Aspartic acid (D)
    • B. Arginine (R)
    • C. Phenylalanine (F)
    • D. Serine (S)
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Phenylalanine is nonpolar and hydrophobic, making it more likely to be buried in the protein’s interior.

  10. What structural level requires more than one polypeptide chain?
    • A. Primary
    • B. Secondary
    • C. Tertiary
    • D. Quaternary
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: Quaternary structure describes the spatial arrangement of multiple polypeptide subunits.

Module 6: Protein Structure & Function

  1. Which parameter is most directly related to activation energy?
    • A. Equilibrium constants
    • B. Reactant concentrations
    • C. Product concentrations
    • D. Rate constants
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: The rate constant (k) is exponentially related to activation energy via the Arrhenius equation.

  2. Which molecule binds to myoglobin with the highest affinity?
    • A. Dioxygen (O₂)
    • B. Carbon monoxide (CO)
    • C. Nitric oxide (NO)
    • D. Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S)
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: NO has the highest Ka (1.4×10¹¹ M⁻¹), indicating strongest binding to myoglobin.

  3. Why does CO bind well to free heme but not to myoglobin?
    • A. CO forms more hydrogen bonds with myoglobin
    • B. Myoglobin destabilizes CO binding via sterics
    • C. CO prefers to bind to metal ions without proteins
    • D. Myoglobin lacks a heme group
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: His E7 in myoglobin causes a steric clash with CO and favors O₂'s bent conformation.

  4. What is the effect of mutating His E7 to Gly in myoglobin?
    • A. Increases CO binding
    • B. Increases O₂ binding
    • C. Decreases O₂ binding
    • D. No effect
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: The His E7 stabilizes O₂ via H-bonding. Removing it weakens O₂ binding affinity.

  5. Which of the following explains the difference in O₂ binding curves of hemoglobin vs. myoglobin?
    • A. Myoglobin has a distal histidine that hemoglobin lacks
    • B. Hemoglobin has subunit communication; myoglobin does not
    • C. Myoglobin binds O₂ cooperatively
    • D. Hemoglobin does not bind O₂ in tissues
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Hemoglobin is a tetramer that exhibits cooperativity through subunit communication.

  6. What is the Hill coefficient (n) for myoglobin?
    • A. 0
    • B. 1
    • C. 2
    • D. 3
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Myoglobin is a monomer and does not exhibit cooperativity (n = 1).

  7. At 30 torr O₂, what is the saturation of myoglobin and hemoglobin, respectively?
    • A. 0.60, 0.91
    • B. 0.91, 0.60
    • C. 2.8 torr, 26 torr
    • D. 0.97, 0.91
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Myoglobin (p50 = 2.8) is nearly saturated at 30 torr; hemoglobin (p50 = 26) is ~60%.

  8. How does increasing pH by 1 unit affect hemoglobin’s T-to-R equilibrium?
    • A. Stabilizes T-state
    • B. Stabilizes R-state
    • C. Has no effect
    • D. Causes denaturation
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Higher pH removes H⁺ from ion pairs, destabilizing T-state and favoring R-state (Bohr effect).

  9. What is the role of BPG (2,3-bisphosphoglycerate) in hemoglobin function?

    • D. Stabilizes R-state to promote O₂ association

    Answer: B
    Explanation: BPG binds the T-state and reduces O₂ affinity, aiding O₂ release in tissues.

  10. How many of the side chain nitrogens in His146 of hemoglobin are protonated at physiological pH?
    • A. 0
    • B. 1
    • C. 2
    • D. 3

    Answer: B
    Explanation: One nitrogen of His is typically protonated near physiological pH, enabling ionic interactions.

Module 7: Catalysis, Rates, and Enzymes

  1. Hydrolysis of peptide bonds has ΔG° = –15 kJ/mol. What does this indicate?
    • A. Reaction is at equilibrium
    • B. Reactant-favored
    • C. Product-favored
    • D. Cannot determine
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: A negative ΔG° means the products (hydrolyzed peptides) are thermodynamically favored.

  2. For 2A + B → C (elementary), what is the rate law?
    • A. Rate = k[A][B]
    • B. Rate = k[B]
    • C. Rate = k[A]²[B]
    • D. Rate = k[A]²
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: In an elementary reaction, the stoichiometry matches the rate law directly.

  3. What are the units of k for Rate = k[A]²[B]?
    • A. s⁻¹
    • B. M⁻¹s⁻¹
    • C. M⁻²s⁻¹
    • D. M·s⁻¹
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: The rate has units of M/s. For 3rd order, k must have units of M⁻²s⁻¹ to balance.

  4. What is the initial velocity if k = 8×10³ s⁻¹ and [A] = 6×10⁻² M (first order)?
    • A. 1.33×10⁵ M/s
    • B. 4.8×10² M/s
    • C. 7.5×10⁻² M/s
    • D. Not enough data
    Show Answer

    Answer: B
    Explanation: Rate = k[A] = (8×10³)(6×10⁻²) = 480 M/s.

  5. Why are intramolecular reactions typically faster than intermolecular ones?
    • A. Increased enthalpy
    • B. Greater ΔG
    • C. Improved proximity and orientation
    • D. Higher pressure
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: The proximity and alignment of groups in intramolecular reactions reduce entropy loss.

  6. Which mutation would most likely reduce chymotrypsin's affinity for substrate?
    • A. Ser189 → F
    • B. Gly216 → V
    • C. Ser189 → G
    • D. Ser189 → A
    Show Answer

    Answer: C
    Explanation: Mutating Ser189 to glycine disrupts the binding pocket that interacts with aromatic residues.

  7. What interactions stabilize the transition state of a serine protease?
    • A. Electrostatic attraction
    • B. Hydrogen bonds
    • C. Electron repulsion
    • D. A and B
    Show Answer

    Answer: D
    Explanation: Electrostatic and H-bond interactions stabilize the negatively charged transition state.

  8. What happens if Asp102 is mutated to Lys in the protease active site?

    • C. Enzyme activity increases

    • D. Allosteric regulator
